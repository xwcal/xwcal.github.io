Jane Street Puzzle solution: Robot Long Jump

2024/02/29

Recently I found in my drawer some five pages of scribble dating back to Mar 2023, of my solution to that month's Jane Street Puzzle 1. Working on the puzzle was a wonderful process of meditation in a time of turmoil, and of reliving the peaceful memories of an aspiring student of math, statistics and computer science 2.

Somewhat surprisingly, I was able to recall the techniques in calculus and probability theory I hadn't touched for years. That must be why I decided to keep the scratch paper: in case I forget that I remembered.

Anyway, since I now have a new power tool I have barely ever used, and which I have just enhanced with numbering and cross-referencing of equations, it's a good time to do some typesetting so that my scribble gets a chance to benefit others.

1 The puzzle

Here is my paraphrase of the problem statement.

You are asked to figure out an optimal strategy to program into a robot that competes against an identical robot (following identical rules) in a long jump game.

In each round, your robot is allowed to run a number of steps along the track before making a beep and taking a leap. The length of each step as well as the leap is an independent random variable drawn from a uniform distribution on [0,1]. (So the "leap" is not really any different from a "step", and would be indistinguishable without a beep.) If before taking the leap, your robot steps beyond the "foul line" at distance 1 from the starting point, then it gets score 0 for that round. Otherwise its score is the final distance from the starting point after the leap.

Your robot and its opponent cannot see each other (or detect each other's moves otherwise).

The game lasts as many rounds as necessary until a round that doesn't end up in a draw. The robot that scores higher for that round wins the game.

To show that you have the correct solution, calculate the probability of your robot scoring 0 in the first round.

2 The solution

2.1 Big steps

Let $\alpha_r$ for $r \in \{0,1\}$ be the leaping thresholds of two robots competing against each other.

For a single round, let $p(F_r|\alpha_r)$ denote the probability of fouling for robot $r$, and let $p(H_r|\alpha_0, \alpha_1)$ be the probability of robot $r$ scoring higher when neither robot fouls, then the probability of robot $r$ winning the round is:

$$p(R_r|\alpha_0, \alpha_1) = p(F_{\neg r}|\alpha_{\neg r})p(\overline{F_r}|\alpha_r) + p(H_r|\alpha_0, \alpha_1)$$(1)

And the probability of the round ending in a draw is:

$$p(D|\alpha_0, \alpha_1) = p(F_r|\alpha_r)p(F_{\neg r}|\alpha_{\neg r})$$(2)

So the probability of robot $r$ winning the game (as a function $\psi$ of $\alpha_0$ and $\alpha_1$) is:

\begin{align*} p(G_r|\alpha_0, \alpha_1) &= \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} p(D|\alpha_0, \alpha_1)^n p(R_r|\alpha_0, \alpha_1) \\ &= \frac{1}{1-p(D|\alpha_0, \alpha_1)} p(R_r|\alpha_0, \alpha_1) \\ &=: \psi_r(\alpha_0, \alpha_1) \end{align*} (3)

Let $\alpha^*$ be optimal, then we have:

$$ \frac{\partial \psi_1(\alpha^*, \beta)} {\partial \beta} \bigg|_{\beta=\alpha^*} = 0 $$(4)

Solve for $\alpha^*$ and then calculate $p(F_1|\alpha^*)$. The result will be the answer we want.

2.2 Small steps

2.2.1 Calculating $p(F_r|\alpha_r)$

For our convenience let's omit the subscript $r$ and add it back later when we need to make distinctions.

Let $X_n$ be the sum of $n$ i.i.d uniform random variables on $[0,1]$. The joint distribution of those random variables is a unit hypercube in $n$ dimensions with density $1$. Using a little geometry, we can derive the CDF (cumulative distribution function): $$ P(X_n < x) = \frac{x^n}{n!} $$(5)

which is the volume of an n-simplex such that each edge on the origin has length $x$. For example, when $n=3$, the volume is just $x^3/6$:

Taking derivative of (5) we obtain the PDF (probability density function): $$ p(x_n) = \frac{x_n^{n-1}}{(n-1)!} $$(6)

The probability of fouling after $n$ steps (for $n \ge 2$) is:

\begin{align*} p(F, n|\alpha) &= P(X_{n-1} < \alpha \land X_n > 1) \\ &= \int_1^2 \int_0^{\alpha} p(x_{n-1}, x_n) dx_{n-1} dx_n \\ &= \int_1^2 \int_0^{\alpha} p(x_{n-1}) p(x_n|x_{n-1}) dx_{n-1} dx_n \\ &= \int_0^{\alpha} p(x_{n-1}) \bigg(\int_1^2 p(x_n|x_{n-1}) dx_n \bigg) dx_{n-1} \\ &= \int_0^{\alpha} \frac{x_{n-1}^{n-2}}{(n-2)!} \bigg(x_{n-1}\bigg) dx_{n-1} \\ &= \frac{\alpha^n}{n(n-2)!} \end{align*} (7)

The probability of fouling can now be worked out using the Taylor expansion of $e^x$ (or rather, its definition): \begin{align*} p(F|\alpha) &= \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} p(F, n|\alpha) \\ &= \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{\alpha^n}{n(n-2)!} \\ &= \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{\alpha^n}{(n-1)!} - \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{\alpha^n}{n!} \\ &= a(e^a-1) - (e^a-a-1) \\ &= ae^a - e^a + 1 \end{align*} (8)

2.2.2 Calculating the score PDF for one robot

The probability of leaping after $n$ steps (without fouling) and getting a score in $[x, x+\delta x]$ is: \begin{align*} p(\overline{F},n,x | \alpha)\delta x &= P(X_{n-1} < \alpha \land X_n \in [\alpha, 1] \land X_{n+1} \in [x, x+\delta x]) \\ &= \int_0^{\alpha} \int_{\alpha}^1 \int_x^{x+\delta x} p(x_{n-1}) p(x_n, x_{n+1} | x_{n-1}) dx_{n+1} dx_n dx_{n-1} \\ &= \int_0^{\alpha} p(x_{n-1}) \bigg( \int_{\alpha}^1 \int_x^{x+\delta x} p(x_n, x_{n+1} | x_{n-1}) dx_{n+1} dx_n \bigg) dx_{n-1} \\ &= \int_0^{\alpha} p(x_{n-1}) \bigg( B(x;\alpha) \delta x \bigg) dx_{n-1} \end{align*} (9) where $B(x; \alpha)$ (as a function of $x$ parametrized by $\alpha$) is an isoseles trapezoidal step function: \begin{align*} B(x;\alpha) \delta x &:= \begin{cases} \int_{\alpha}^x \int_x^{x+\delta x} 1 dx_{n+1} dx_n & x \in [\alpha, 1) \\ \int_{\alpha}^1 \int_x^{x+\delta x} 1 dx_{n+1} dx_n & x \in [1, 1 + \alpha) \\ \int_{x-1}^1 \int_x^{x+\delta x} 1 dx_{n+1} dx_n & x \in [1 + \alpha, 2) \end{cases} \\ &= \begin{cases} (x-\alpha)\delta x & x \in [\alpha, 1) \\ (1-\alpha)\delta x & x \in [1, 1 + \alpha) \\ (2-x) \delta x & x \in [1 + \alpha, 2) \end{cases} \end{align*} (10)

Combining equations (6) and (9) and sum over $n$, we obtain the score PDF for a single robot: \begin{align*} p(\overline{F},x | \alpha) &= \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} p(\overline{F},n,x | \alpha) \\ &= \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{a^{n-1}}{(n-1)!} B(x;\alpha) \\ &= e^{\alpha} B(x;\alpha) \end{align*} (11)

2.2.3 Fun with $p(H_r|\alpha_0, \alpha_1)$ and its partial derivative

Let's think about it: if two competing robots both employ the optimal strategy, ie. we have $\alpha_0 = \alpha_1 = \alpha^*$, then the probability of not fouling and scoring higher than the opponent should be the same: $$ p(H_0|\alpha_*, \alpha_*) = p(H_1|\alpha_*, \alpha_*) = \frac{1}{2} p(\overline{F}|\alpha^*)^2 $$(12) where $p(\overline{F}|\alpha) = 1- p(F|\alpha)$ as we derived in (8).

Easy!

But the partial derivative is not easy3.

Let's start by writing down: $$ p(H_1|\alpha_0, \alpha_1) = \iint \displaylimits_{x \in [\alpha_0, 2] \land y \in [\alpha_1, 2] \land x \le y} e^{\alpha_0}e^{\alpha_1} B(x;\alpha_0) B(y;\alpha_1) dx dy $$(13) where the join PDF of two scores is simply two individial score pdfs multiplied together because of independence.

We'd like to take: $$ \frac{\partial p(H_1|\alpha^*, \alpha_1)} {\partial \alpha_1} \bigg|_{\alpha_1=\alpha^*} = 0 $$(14)

But how do we deal with this awkward "platform" integrand $e^{\alpha_0}e^{\alpha_1} B(x;\alpha_0) B(y;\alpha_1)$?

It's easier if we look at the situation from above. The integral $p(H_1|\alpha_*, \alpha_*)$ is the volume over the shaded area (each cross marks the $(x,y)$ coordinate of the corresponding corner of the top surface of the "platform"):

When we change the second argument by a small amount $\delta \alpha$, the integral becomes $p(H_1|\alpha^*, \alpha^* + \delta \alpha)$, which is the volume over the shaded area in the updated view on the right:

This looks forbidding!

Fortunately there is a way to avoid evaluating it directly.

The transformation from $p(H_1|\alpha_*, \alpha_*)$ to $p(H_1|\alpha^*, \alpha^* + \delta \alpha)$ can be seen as a two step process.

First, it proceeds from $p(H_1|\alpha_*, \alpha_*)$ to $(1/2) p(\overline{F}|\alpha^*)p(\overline{F}|\alpha^* + \delta \alpha)$, which is the volume over the shaded area on the right (note how the new "platform" is evenly split by the $\int$ shaped divider):

Remembering (12) above, we can easily obtain the Step 1 difference: \begin{align*} &\frac{1}{2} p(\overline{F}|\alpha^*)p(\overline{F}|\alpha^* + \delta \alpha) - p(H_1|\alpha_*, \alpha_*) \\ &= \frac{1}{2} p(\overline{F}|\alpha^*) \bigg(p(\overline{F}|\alpha^* + \delta \alpha) - p(\overline{F}|\alpha^*)\bigg) \\ &= \frac{1}{2} p(\overline{F}|\alpha^*) \bigg(\frac{\partial p(\overline{F}|\alpha)}{\partial \alpha}\bigg|_{\alpha = \alpha^*} \delta \alpha \bigg) + \mathcal{O}(\delta \alpha^2) \\ &= - \frac{1}{2} \alpha^* e^{\alpha^*} (e^{\alpha^*} - \alpha^* e^{\alpha^*}) \delta \alpha + \mathcal{O}(\delta \alpha^2) \end{align*} (15)

Next, the transformation proceeds from $(1/2) p(\overline{F}|\alpha^*)p(\overline{F}|\alpha^* + \delta \alpha)$ to $p(H_1|\alpha^*, \alpha^* + \delta \alpha)$:

The difference is the volume over the highlighted areas:

Or, we can look at the difference back in 3D:

After taking a close look at this piece of art (or garbage, depending on taste), some might raise concerns over the area A1 in the 3D figure: "You added a little triangle that wasn't there before!"

That's right. The little extra base area is needed so that the volume over A1 is exactly half of the volume over A3. And this is completely legal: the added volume is of $\mathcal{O}(\delta \alpha^2)$ and therefore goes away eventually (we will get there soon).

Using a little calculus and geometry, we can work out the volumes: \begin{align*} V_{A1} &= e^{2 \alpha^*} \frac{1}{3} \bigg( \int_{\alpha^*}^1 (\sqrt{2}dx)(x-\alpha^*)^2 \bigg) (\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \delta \alpha) + \mathcal{O}(\delta \alpha^2)\\ &= e^{2 \alpha^*} \frac{1}{9} (1-\alpha^*)^3 \delta \alpha + \mathcal{O}(\delta \alpha^2)\\ \\ V_{A2} &= e^{2 \alpha^*} \frac{1}{2} \alpha^* (1-\alpha^*)^2 \delta \alpha \\ \\ V_{A3} &= 2 V_{A1} \\ &= e^{2 \alpha^*} \frac{2}{9} (1-\alpha^*)^3 \delta \alpha + \mathcal{O}(\delta \alpha^2) \end{align*} (16)

Adding the three Vs together we arrive at the Step 2 difference: \begin{align*} & p(H_1|\alpha^*, \alpha^* + \delta \alpha) - \frac{1}{2} p(\overline{F}|\alpha^*)p(\overline{F}|\alpha^* + \delta \alpha)\\ &= e^{2 \alpha^*} (\frac{(1-\alpha^*)^3}{3} + \frac{\alpha^* (1-\alpha^*)^2}{2}) \delta \alpha + \mathcal{O}(\delta \alpha^2) \end{align*} (17)

Finally, combine the Step 1 difference in (15) and the Step 2 difference in (17), divide by $\delta \alpha$, and take the limit to get: \begin{align*} & \frac{\partial p(H_1|\alpha^*, \alpha_1)} {\partial \alpha_1} \bigg|_{\alpha_1=\alpha^*} \\ &= \lim_{\delta \alpha \to 0} \frac{p(H_1|\alpha^*, \alpha^* + \delta \alpha) - p(H_1|\alpha_*, \alpha_*)}{\delta \alpha} \\ &= - \frac{1}{2} \alpha^* e^{\alpha^*} (e^{\alpha^*} - \alpha^* e^{\alpha^*}) + e^{2 \alpha^*} (\frac{(1-\alpha^*)^3}{3} + \frac{\alpha^* (1-\alpha^*)^2}{2})) \end{align*} (18)

2.3 Final steps

We are now ready to return to the big steps and fill in the details.

Expanding the partial derivative in (4) and set the numerator to zero (the denominator is a square) we get: \begin{align*} & \bigg( p(F|\alpha^*) \frac{\partial p(\overline{F}|\beta)}{\beta} \bigg|_{\beta = \alpha^*} + \frac{\partial p(H_1|\alpha^*, \beta)} {\partial \beta} \bigg|_{\beta=\alpha^*} \bigg) (1 - p(F|\alpha^*)^2) \\ &= \bigg( p(F|\alpha^*)p(\overline{F}|\alpha^*) + p(H_1|\alpha^*, \alpha^*) \bigg) p(F|\alpha^*) \frac{\partial p(F|\beta)}{\beta} \bigg|_{\beta = \alpha^*} \end{align*} (19)

Substitute the results above, factor out $(\alpha^* - 1) e^{\alpha^*} (\alpha^* e^{\alpha^*} - e^{\alpha^*} + 2)$ from both sides, and rearrange to get: $$ 3\alpha^* = e^{\alpha^*} ({\alpha^*}^3 - 3\alpha^* + 2) $$(20)

Solve for $\alpha^* \in (0,1)$ and we get 0.416195354858236223453873025....

Substitute into $p(F|\alpha^*)$ to obtain the answer: 0.114845885981340779751893743316... (Jane Street needs only 9 digits but we can splurge a little after all the hardwork that gave us this almost free digit generator).

3 Validation

I am not T-800 and there is no guarentee that I made no mistake (I should say if I were to do this again from scratch some mistakes would be guarenteed). So some validation is needed.

Here is a simple-minded python script:

from random import random, seed

SEED = 100

ALPHA_REF = 0.416195354858236223453873025
ALPHA_ALTERS = [0.38, 0.39, 0.4, 0.41, 0.42, 0.43, 0.44, 0.45, 0.46]

class Robot:
    def __init__(self, alpha):
        self._alpha = alpha

    def run(self):
        x = 0
        alpha = self._alpha
        while x < alpha:
            x += random()
        if x < 1:
            # jump!
            return x + random()
        else:
            # foul!
            return 0

def game(bot0, bot1):
    scr0 = bot0.run()
    scr1 = bot1.run()
    if scr0 > scr1:
        return 0
    elif scr0 < scr1:
        return 1
    else:
        return game(bot0, bot1)

def compete(ngames, bot0, bot1):
    winct0 = 0
    winct1 = 0
    for _ in range(ngames):
        if game(bot0, bot1) == 0:
            winct0 += 1
        else:
            winct1 += 1
    return winct0 / winct1

def verify(ngames, alpha_ref, alpha_alters):
    '''
    verify alpha_ref is optimal against alpha_alters
    '''
    seed(SEED)
    botref = Robot(alpha_ref)
    for alpha in alpha_alters:
        print('ref %.9f vs %.9f'%(alpha_ref, alpha))
        bot = Robot(alpha)
        winratio = compete(ngames, bot, botref)
        if winratio <= 1:
            print('Good: %.5f'%winratio)
        else:
            print('FAIL: %.5f'%winratio)

4 Universal optimality

All the work above should be sufficient to make most people happy. But if you are like me, you probably still wonder: have we missed any possibilities?

An important assumption we made is that the optimal strategy must be of the kind: "jump after threshold $\alpha$". But what if there are better strategies of other kinds? How about something like "jump after five steps", or "toss a die and then jump after the corresponding number of steps"?

If we think about it, all possible strategies can be cast into the kind that we shall call variable threshold strategies (which includes our fixed threshold strategies as a subkind), with the variable threshold $T$ being where the robot lands after taking the last step.

In the most general case, $T$ is a random variable that is the output of a policy function $\pi$ with two arguments: a uniform random variable on $[0,1]$ and the entire world history known to the robot up to the moment the decision is made.

How $\pi$ decides $T$ is not important. $T$ has some probability distribution $p(t)$ we don't need to know. All we care about is this (dropping irrelevant variables): $$ p(G|\alpha^*) \ge p(G|t), \forall t \in [0,\infty) $$(21)

which holds because of the optimality of $\alpha^*$, and therefore: $$ p(G|\alpha^*) \ge \int_{0}^{\infty} p(G|t)p(t)dt $$(22)

So, restricting our attention to fixed threshold strategies doesn't cause us to miss anything better.
