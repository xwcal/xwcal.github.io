2 Mathematical prerequisites

This section is a structured speedrun through a considerable number of topics and is meant for those who have already had some exposure to rigorous mathematics and prefer not to sift through the collages on Wikipedia.

Impatient readers should feel free to jump to the application and come back if necessary.

2.1 Groups

2.1.1 Definition

A set $G$ with a function $*: G \times G \rightarrow G$ is a group if:

  • $*$ is associative: For all $a,b,c \in G$, $(a*b)*c = a*(b*c)$;
  • There is an identity element $e \in G$, such that for all $a \in G$, $e*a = a$ and $a*e = a$;
  • For all $a \in G$, there is an inverse element $b \in G$, such that $a*b = e$ and $b*a = e$.

$*$ is called the group operation, $G$ is called the underlying set, and these three rules are called the group axioms.

2.1.2 Some properties

From the definition, we can immediately draw the following conclusions:

2.1.2.1 Proposition

The identity element is unique.

Proof

Suppose $e$ and $e'$ are identities, then $e = e*e' = e'$ ∎

2.1.2.2 Proposition

For each $a \in G$, there is a unique inverse element.

Proof

Suppose $b$ and $c$ are inverses of $a$, then \begin{align*} b &= e*b \\ &= (c*a)*b \\ &= c*(a*b) \\ &= c*e \\ &= c \end{align*} (1)∎

(Observe how the various group axioms are employed at different steps to give us this equality.)

Since the inverse of each group element is unique, we can define a function $(\cdot)^{-1}: G \rightarrow G$ that takes each element to its inverse.

2.1.3 Examples

Don't be deterred by the abstractness. I bet we are all familiar with several different groups.

The set of integers $\mathbb{Z}$ is a group, with addition being the group operation. $0$ is the identity element, and for any integer $a$, its inverse element is $-a$.

The set of rational numbers without $0$, ie. $\mathbb{Q}-\{0\}$, is a group, with multiplication being the group operation. $1$ is the identity element, and for a nonzero rational $a$, its inverse element is $\frac{1}{a}$ (or if you prefer, $a^{-1}$, which explains the common notation for an inverse element).

The set of real numbers $\mathbb{R}$ gives us two groups (which two?).

2.1.4 Subgroups

Given a group $G$ with operation $*$, a subgroup $H$ is a subset of $G$ such that:

  • The identity element of $G$ is in $H$;
  • $H$ is closed under $*$: for $a,b \in H$, $a*b \in H$;
  • $H$ is closed under $(\cdot)^{-1}$: for $a \in H$, $a^{-1} \in H$.

These conditions ensure that $H$ with $*$ restricted to $H \times H$ is a group.

2.1.5 Cyclic groups

For $g \in G$, a cyclic subgroup generated by $g$ is the set $\{g^n \in G| n \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ 2 with the same group operation, where $g^n$ denotes $\underbrace{g * g * \dots g}_{\text{n times}}$ for $n > 0$, with $g^0 := e$ and $g^{-n} := (g^{-1})^n$.

It should be easy to verify that this indeed satisfies the definition of a subgroup.

A group is cyclic if it can be generated by a single element, called its generator.

2.1.6 Abelian groups

A group $G$ is abelian if $*$ is commutative, ie. for $a,b \in G$, $a*b = b*a$.

It's not hard to show that a cyclic group must also be abelian.

2.1.7 More examples

$\mathbb{Q}$ with addition is a group. $\mathbb{Z}$ is a subgroup. The set of even integers (denoted $2\mathbb{Z}$) is a subgroup of the subgroup (and also a subgroup of $\mathbb{Q}$). The set of non-negative integers is not a subgroup, because it's not closed under $(\cdot)^{-1}$.

$\mathbb{Z}$ is cyclic: every element is a multiple of $1$. $\mathbb{Q}$ is not cyclic.

All these groups are abelian, but certainly not all groups are abelian (most aren't).

Consider the set of $2 \times 2$ real matrices with multiplication as the group operation. Let's check 3: $$ \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1\\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2\\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 4\\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} $$(2) where as $$ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2\\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1\\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1\\ 4 & 3 \end{bmatrix} $$(3)

2.2 Rings

2.2.1 Definition

Suppose $R$ is an abelian group with operation $+$ and identity element $0$, then $R$ is a ring if it has another operation $\cdot: R \times R \rightarrow R$, such that:

  • $\cdot$ is associative: for all $a, b, c \in R$, $(a \cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c)$;
  • There is a multiplicative identity $1 \in R$ such that for all $a \in R$, $1 \cdot a = a$ and $a \cdot 1 = a$.
  • $\cdot$ distributes over $+$: for $a,b,c \in R$, $a\cdot(b+c) = a\cdot b + a\cdot c$ and $(b+c)\cdot a = b\cdot a + c\cdot a$

These along with the groups axioms and commutativity of $+$ are called the ring axioms.

In the context of rings, we refer to $0$ as the additive identity, $+$ as addition, and $\cdot$ as multiplication. The inverse of $a$ with respect to $+$ is denoted $-a$ (which is a common practice when we think of the group operation as "addition" instead of "multiplication").

A prototypical example of a ring is $\mathbb{Z}$ with the usual operations of addition and multiplication. Another (IMO) more interesting but less useful example is the "trivial ring" $\{0\}$. Interested readers can verify that (with inevitable definitions of the operations and identities) it's indeed a ring. It is the only ring where the additive identity equals the multiplicative identity, as we will show next.

2.2.2 Some properties

2.2.2.1 Proposition

For all $a \in R$, $a \cdot 0 = 0$ and $0 \cdot a = 0$.

Proof

\begin{align*} a \cdot 0 &= 0 + a \cdot 0 \\ &= (-(a \cdot 0) + a \cdot 0) + a \cdot 0 \\ &= -(a \cdot 0) + (a \cdot 0 + a \cdot 0) \\ &= -(a \cdot 0) + a \cdot (0 + 0) \\ &= -(a \cdot 0) + a \cdot 0 \\ &= 0 \end{align*} (4)

The other equality is similar. ∎

While we probably all know this as an "axiom" of some sort from as far back as elementary school, observe how it follows from the ring axioms without having to be an axiom itself.

2.2.2.2 Proposition

For all $a \in R$, $-a = (-1) \cdot a$.

Proof

We already know the inverse is unique, so we just need to show $(-1) \cdot a$ does the job of an inverse: \begin{align*} (-1) \cdot a + a &= (-1) \cdot a + 1 \cdot a \\ &= (-1 + 1) \cdot a \\ &= 0 \cdot a \\ &= 0 \end{align*} (5) ∎

2.2.2.3 Proposition

If $1 = 0$ in a ring $R$, then it's the trivial ring.

Proof

For $a \in R$, we have $a = a \cdot 1 = a \cdot 0 = 0$ (by 2.2.2.1). ∎

2.3 Equivalence classes

2.3.1 Definitions

Given a set $S$ and a relation $\sim \subseteq S \times S$, $\sim$ is an equivalence relation if:

  • It is reflexive: for $a \in S$, $a \sim a$;
  • It is symmetric: for $a, b \in S$, $a \sim b$ if and only if $b \sim a$;
  • It is transitive: for $a, b, c \in S$, if $a \sim b$ and $b \sim c$ then $a \sim c$.

Given an equivalence relation $\sim$ on $S$ and $a \in S$, we can define a set $\{b \in S| b \sim a\}$, ie. one that contains exactly all the elements equivalent to $a$. This set is called an equivalence class, denoted $[a]$, with $a$ being the representative. The set of all such equivalence classes is commonly denoted $S/\sim$.

Two distinct equivalence classes are necessarily disjoint, for suppose we have $c \in [a]$ and $c \in [b]$, then $a \sim c \sim b$, and by transitivity, $a \sim b$, so $[a] = [b]$. We say the equivalence classes partition $S$. Conversely, given a partitioning of $S$ into disjoint subsets, we can define an equivalence relation by stipulating $a \sim b$ if $a$ and $b$ belong to the same partition.

Equivalence classes appear throughout mathematics, from elementary number theory (as we shall see next) to forcing (take a guess what it means 4). Arguably there wouldn't be mathematics without equivalence classes. That's why the topic is covered here in preparation for modular arithmetic, although we could probably talk about the latter without mentioning equivalence classes.

2.3.2 Examples

Given a set $S$, $=$ is a prototypical equivalence relation. And for each $a \in S$, $[a] = \{a\}$.

We can also regard $S$ as a single partition, and this gives us another equivalence relation such that every two elements are equivalent.

2.4 Number Theory

2.4.1 Euclidean division lemma

For $a,b \in \mathbb{Z}$, $b \neq 0$, there are unique integers $q$ and $r$ such that:

  • $a = qb + r$
  • $0 \le r < |b|$

We take this lemma for granted without a proof.

The unique (given $a$ and $b$) integers have established names: $q$ is called the quotient and $r$ is called the remainder. We also write $a \bmod{b}$ to refer to the remainder without naming it. In case the remainder is $0$, we say $b$ divides $a$, or $b|a$. When $a \bmod{c} = b \bmod{c}$, we write $a \equiv b \pmod c$.

2.4.2 Some properties

Let the variables denote integers (nonzero when necessary); these are some immediate consequences of the lemma and definitions:

2.4.2.1 Proposition

$b|a$ iff $b|(-a)$. 5

Proof

$a = qb$ iff $-a = (-q)b$. ∎

2.4.2.2 Proposition

$a \equiv b \pmod c$ iff $c|(a-b)$.

Proof

Suppose $a \equiv b \pmod c$, then there are $p, q$ and $r$ such that $a = p c + r$ and $b = q c + r$ per 2.4.1. So $a-b = (p - q)c$. Thus $c|(a-b)$.

On the other hand, suppose $a \not\equiv b \pmod c$, there are $p, q$ and $r \neq s$ such that $a = p c + r$ and $b = q c + s$ per 2.4.1. If $0 \le s < r < |c|$, then $a-b = (p-q)c + (r-s)$ where $0 < r-s < |c|$, so by uniqueness $c \not| (a-b)$. Whereas if $0 \le r < s < |c|$, we can infer $c \not| (b-a)$, and by 2.4.2.1 $c \not| (a-b)$

∎

2.4.2.3 Proposition

If $d|a$, then $d|ca$ for any $c$.

Proof

If $a=qd$, then $ca = (cq)d$. ∎

2.4.2.4 Proposition

If $d|a$ and $d|b$, then $d|(a+b)$.

Proof

If $a = pd$ and $b = qd$, then $a+b = (p+q)d$. ∎

2.4.2.5 Proposition

If $a \equiv b \pmod d$ and $b \equiv c \pmod d$, then $a \equiv c \pmod d$.

Proof

Just apply 2.4.2.2 and 2.4.2.4. ∎

2.4.2.6 Proposition

If $a_1 \equiv a_2 \pmod c$ and $b_1 \equiv b_2 \pmod c$, then $a_1 + b_1 \equiv a_2 + b_2 \pmod c$ and $a_1 b_1 \equiv a_2 b_2 \pmod c$.

Proof

From the antecedent we have, for $i \in \{1,2\}$, $a_i = p_i c + r$ and $b_i = q_i c + s$ per 2.4.1 and thus $a_i + b_i \equiv r+s \pmod c$ by 2.4.2.2. The consequent $a_1 + b_1 \equiv a_2 + b_2 \pmod c$ follows from 2.4.2.5. The case for multiplication is similar. ∎

2.4.3 Greatest common divisor

For $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}$, define $gcd(a,b)$ as the greatest integer that divides both $a$ and $b$.

Clearly a gcd is always positive, since $1$ is always a common divisor. Also, if $b > 0$ and $b|a$ then $gcd(a,b) = b$.

2.4.3.1 Proposition

For $a,b,q,r \in \mathbb{Z}$ such that $a = bq+r$, $gcd(a,b) = gcd(b,r)$.

Proof

If $d|a$ and $d|b$, then $d|(a-bq)$ by 2.4.2.3 and 2.4.2.4, and thus $d|r$. Similarly, if $d|b$ and $d|r$ then $d|a$.∎

2.4.4 Bézout's identity

For $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}$ such that $gcd(a.b) = d$, there exist $x,y \in \mathbb{Z}$ such that $ax + by = d$.

Proof

By symmetry, we only need to consider the restricted senario where $a \ge b > 0$. By 2.4.1 we have $a = qb + r$ such that $0 \le r < b$. In case $r = 0$, we have $a+(1-q)b = d$. Otherwise, by 2.4.3.1 and induction hypothesis, there are $x$ and $y$ such that $bx + ry = d$. Substitute $r = a-qb$ and rearrange we get $ay+b(x-qy) = d$. ∎

For those who have ventured far into mathematics but not further, the proof may look dubious: what kind of induction is that?

It's called well-founded induction. We have implicitly used the fact that $(a_1, b_1) < (a_2, b_2) :\Leftrightarrow a_2 = b_1 \land a_1 = qb_1 + b_2$ (where quotient $q$ and remainder $b_2$ are given by 2.4.1) is a well-founded relation on $\{(a,b) \in \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}| a \ge b > 0\}$.

I have never seen a proof of Bézout's identity using well-founded induction, probably because the latter is never taught in the context of elementary number theory (and I bet plenty of mathematicians don't even know about it). With this "black magic", the proof has been made considerably short (compare it with, say, the proof on Wikipedia). This is how math gets "compressed" as we make progress. 2.5 Modular arithmetic

2.5.1 $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$

Fix a positive integer $m$, it's straightforward to verify (we have already proved transitivity) that $a \equiv b \pmod m$ is an equivalence relation. It partitions $\mathbb{Z}$ into $m$ equivalence classes such that for each $r \in \mathbb{Z}, 0 \le r < m$, the equivalence class $[r]$ contains exactly those integers that yield remainder $r$ when divided by $m$. The set of these equivalence classes is denoted $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$. This warrants a little digression since it's a nice illustration of math as an art of notation.

2.5.2 Quotient sets

The "positive" restriction in the previous subsection is unnecessary (but convenient so we keep it). In fact, for any integer $m$, including $0$, we can define $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$ without an issue.

First, let $m\mathbb{Z}$ denote the set $\{ma | a \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ (yeah this is how we "multiply" an integer and a set of integers). This gives us, for example, the set of even numbers when $m=2$, the set of multiples of $3$ when $m=3$, and so on. The special case, $0\mathbb{Z}$, is simply $\{0\}$. Moreover, $m\mathbb{Z}$ and $(-m)\mathbb{Z}$ are one and the same, since $ma = (-m)(-a)$.

Then, define $a \sim b$ if $a-b \in m\mathbb{Z}$. It's easy to check using 2.4.2.2 that for $m > 0$, $a-b \in m\mathbb{Z}$ iff $a \equiv b \pmod m$, so we get the same equivalence relation as in the previous subsection.

Since $m\mathbb{Z}$ and $(-m)\mathbb{Z}$ are the same, restricting $m$ to be positive doesn't cause any loss of generality, that is, except when $m=0$. But what do we get when $m=0$? In this special case not covered by the previous subsection, $\sim$ reduces to $=$, so each equivalence class is just a singleton set, and $\mathbb{Z}/0\mathbb{Z}$ looks and behaves much like $\mathbb{Z}$.

Those meeting $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$ for the first time probably wonder if the "$/$" actually suggests anything. Well, it does! Picture the Cartesian product $m\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$: if we label the y-axis with the remainders associated with the equivalence classes, then for each cell $(mq, r)$ there is a corresponding $a \in \mathbb{Z}$ (the $a = mq+r$) and vice versa. So $m\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$ is like $\mathbb{Z}$ rearranged into multiple rows, and "$/$" is like the inverse operation of "$\times$". In other words, $/$ is what it means: quotient. That's why when we partition a set into equivalence classes using an equivalence relation, the resulting set of equivalence classes is also called a quotient set.

2.5.3 $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$ as a cyclic group

2.5.3.1 The group operation

In order to be considered a group, $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$ needs a group operation. We'd like to think of it as "addition" for reasons that will be obvious soon, so let's define $+$ as:

$$[a] + [b] = [a+b]$$(6)

This definition is interesting in several ways. First note that we are overloading the $+$ operator: the LHS $+$ has a different meaning than the RHS $+$. Now remember equivalence classes are sets, so we are defining addition on sets in terms of addition on integers 7. But, despite the complexity underneath, the apparence is mysteriously simple: if the square brackets weren't there, we'd get an undeniable equality: $a+b = a+b$.

The apparence is misleading, because there is a caveat: an equivalence class can be written using different representatives. For example, if $m=3$, then $[2]$ refers to the same equivalence class as $[5]$. If we were to dream up a "function", say $f([a]) = [a \bmod{2}]$, the "function" wouldn't work: $[2 \bmod{2}] = [0] \neq [1] = [5 \bmod{2}]$. In other words, although $[2] = [5]$, $f([2]) \neq f([5])$ ! This is an example of a "function" that's not well-defined.

Fortunately, the group operation in this case is well-defined: 2.4.2.6 is just a different way of saying if $[a_1] = [a_2]$ and $[b_1] = [b_2]$, then $[a_1 + a_2] = [b_1 + b_2]$.

We also need to verify $+$ is associative:

\begin{align*} ([a]+[b])+[c] &= [a+b]+[c] \\ &= [(a+b)+c] \\ &= [a+(b+c)] \\ &= [a]+[b+c] \\ &= [a]+([b]+[c]) \end{align*} (7) Note how the associativity of integer addition gives rise to the associativity of the group operation. This is a recurrent theme in math where we often work with things that "look like" each other.

The identity element is unsurprising: $[0]$. So is the inverse of $[a]$: $[-a]$. We are now confident that $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$ is indeed a group with the operation we just defined.

2.5.3.2 The cyclicity

Since we are thinking of the group operation as "addition", we need to vary the notation in 2.1.5: for $n \in \mathbb{Z}$ and $[a] \in \mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$, define $n[a] = [na]$ (check that it's well-defined). This gives us, among other things, a concise expression for $\underbrace{[a] + [a] + \dots [a]}_{\text{n times}}$ when $n > 0$.

To prove that $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$ is cyclic, all we need to do is to find a generator. But look no further than $[1]$. Indeed, for $[a] \in \mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$, we have $[a] = a[1]$, so everything can be generated by $[1]$.

I previously mentioned $\mathbb{Z}$ as an example of a cyclic group. That example doesn't do much justice to the word "cyclic" (well, it's not cyclic in the usual sense). We now have better examples.

Let $m=12$, we get a cyclic group we all know since childhood: the clock.

Twelve o'clock is $[12]$, which is equal to $[0]$. We add one hour at a time, and progress through $[1]$ (one o'clock), $[2]$ (two o'clock) … Eventually, after adding an hour to $[11]$, we are back to where we started: twelve o'clock!

The minutes and seconds are another cyclic group: $\mathbb{Z}/60\mathbb{Z}$.

In fact, every cyclic group is "isomorphic"8 to either $\mathbb{Z}$ or $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$ for some $m$. So to understand cyclic groups, all we need to do is to study $\mathbb{Z}$ and $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$.

2.5.4 Generators and the order of a cyclic group

We picked $[1]$ as the obvious generator to show the cyclicity of $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$. Without a doubt $[1]$ works for all $m$, but it is not the only choice.

Consider $\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}$ as an example.

If we calculate $n[5]$ for $n = 0, 1, \cdots, 11$, we get the following sequence: $[0]$, $[5]$, $[10]$, $[3]$, $[8]$, $[1]$, $[6]$, $[11]$, $[4]$, $[9]$, $[2]$, and $[7]$, which are exactly the 12 elements of $\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}$. So $[5]$ is a generator of $\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}$.

However, $[3]$ is not a generator of $\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}$, since no matter how many $n$'s we iterate through, $n[3]$ only allows us to visit these four elements: $[0]$, $[3]$, $[6]$ and $[9]$. In fact, what we get is a cyclic subgroup generated by $[3]$ and consisting of exactly those four elements.

We call the size of a group $G$ its order. We also conveniently talk about the order of a generator in a similar sense, denoted $ord(g)$ for $g \in G$, by which we mean the size of the subgroup generated by $g$. In the examples above, $\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}$ has order 12, $ord([5]) = 12$ and $ord([3]) = 4$.

2.5.4.1 Proposition

For $[d] \in \mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$ such that $d > 0$ and $d|m$, we have $ord([d]) = m/d$.

Proof

For $n \in \{0, \cdots, (m/d)-1\}$, we have $0 \le nd < m$, so $S := \{n[d]| n = 0, \cdots, (m/d)-1\}$ contains exactly $m/d$ elements due to remainder uniqueness in 2.4.1. The same gives us, for $n' \in \mathbb{Z}$, some $q$ and $r$ such that $n' = q(m/d) + r$ and $0 \le r < m/d$. Multiply by $d$ and we get $n'd = qm + rd$. By 2.4.2.2, we have $[n'd] = [rd]$, and thus $n'[d] =r[d] \in S$. This shows that $S$ is the subgroup generated by $[d]$. It follows that $ord([d]) = m/d$. ∎

2.5.4.2 Proposition

Given $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$ and $a \in \mathbb{Z}$, we have $ord([a]) = m/gcd(a, m)$.

Proof

Let $d = gcd(a, m)$, then we have, by 2.4.4, $x$ and $y$ such that $ax + my = d$, and thus by 2.4.2.2, $[ax] = [d]$. Factor out $x$ as in 2.5.3.2 and we get $[d] = x[a]$. Moreover, since $d|a$, we have $[a] = [qd] = q[d]$ for some $q$. Since $[a]$ and $[d]$ generate each other, they generate the same subgroup of $ord([d])$, which equals $m/d$ per 2.5.4.1.∎

2.5.4.3 Proposition

Given $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$ and $a \in \mathbb{Z}$, we have $ord([a])[a] = [0]$.

Proof

Let $d = gcd(a, m)$, then by 2.4.1 $a = qd$ for some $q$, and by 2.5.4.2 $ord([a]) = m/d$. Thus: \begin{align*} ord([a])[a] &= (m/d)[qd] \\ &= [(m/d)qd] \\ &= q[m] \\ &= q[0] \\ &= [0] \end{align*} (8) ∎

2.5.4.4 Proposition

Given $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$ and $a,b \in \mathbb{Z}$ such that $0 < b < ord([a])$, we have $b[a] \neq [0]$.

Proof

Let $d = gcd(a,m)$ and $s = ord([a])$. As in the proof of 2.5.4.2, $ord([d]) = s$ as well, and there is $x \in \mathbb{Z}$ such that $x[a] = [d]$. As in the proof of 2.5.4.1, $S:=\{n[d]|n = 0, \cdots, s-1\}$ has $s$ distinct elements. If for $0 < b < s$ we had $b[a] = [0]$, we would have $b[d] = xb[a] = [0] = 0[d]$, contradicting the distinctness of elements in $S$. Thus $b[a] \neq [0]$ as desired. ∎

2.5.5 $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$ as a ring

We define multiplication as:

$$ [a] \cdot [b] = [ab] $$(9)

which is just like equation 6 (that is, if we keep the dot when we write integer multiplication).

The verification is done just like before, with an additional step to check distributivity similar to derivation 7. $[1]$ is the multiplicative identity.

The cyclicity means $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$ is abelian, so we now have a ring.

With multiplication, we can also define exponentiation as $[a]^n = [a^n]$, which will be useful next.

2.5.6 Digit sum

Last year I had some "fun" on CodeSignal. A problem I encountered was something like this:

Given a positive integer that's not a multiple of 9, sum its digits. If the result is a single digit, done. Otherwise, repeat until the result is a single digit.

The statement obviously prescribes an algorithm involving either recursion or iteration. But for those who know the trick the answer is even simpler: n mod 9 is all it takes!

For example, if we start with 1970 and follow the statement to the letter, we get the reduction sequence: 1970 -> 17 -> 8. But 8 is exactly 1970 mod 9. We can also try something bigger. Starting with, say, 4294967296, we get 4294967296 -> 58 -> 13 -> 4. But 4294967296 mod 9 also equals 4.

Magic!

Well, what the question is asking for is called the digital root. This is also what is calculated in step 3 of the procedure in Daniel's post at the very beginning.

Daniel has already talked about why the digital root is the same as the remainder modulo 9. But 2.5.5 gives us a simpler explanation:

The remainder is digit sum invariant.

To see this, simply note that in $\mathbb{Z}/9\mathbb{Z}$, $[10] = [1]$, which is just the multiplicative identity. Thus we have, for example: \begin{align*} [1970] &= [1 \cdot 10^3 + 9 \cdot 10^2 + 7 \cdot 10 + 0] \\ &= [1] \cdot [10]^3 + [9] \cdot [10]^2 + [7] \cdot [10] + [0] \\ &= [1] + [9] + [7] + [0] \\ &= [1+9+7+0] \end{align*} (10) which is just another way of saying $1970 \bmod{9} = (1+9+7+0) \bmod{9}$.

Of course 9 is really just like any other base, so the same conclusion holds regardless of what base we choose.

3 From theory to application

3.1 The algorithm

Having looked at groups and rings, and most importantly, $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$ as a cyclic group and its generators and subgroups, we are now ready to study the algorithm given at the beginning in greater detail.

In pseudocode, the ideal algorithm behind the demo (implemented with additional limitations for practical reasons) can be summarized as:

Input:
$ord \in \mathbb{Z}, ord > 0$
$gen \in \mathbb{Z}, 0 < gen < ord$
$angle \in \mathbb{Q}, 0 < angle < 180$

State variables:
$point \in \mathbb{R}^2$:
    current location of the drawing head
    initially $(0,0)$
$dir \in \mathbb{Q}$:
    current direction in degrees
    initially $0$

Variable for convenience:
$len \in \mathbb{Z}$
    length of the next line segment


If not ACCEPTABLE($ord$, $gen$, $angle$):
    Abort
For $n$ in range$(0, \infty)$:
    Set $len = (n \cdot gen) \bmod{ord}$
    If $len = 0$:
        Continue
    Draw a line segment of $len$ in $dir$
    Update $point$
    Increment $dir$ by $angle$ modulo $360$
    If loop has closed:
        Finish

The original algorithm presented in Daniel's post credited to his former headmaster requires calculating the digital root. But as shown above this is the same as taking the remainder modulo 9 (or whatever base we prefer), so we work with ord/modulus directly.

Besides varying the base, Daniel has also generalized the angle, which is fixed to 90 degrees in the original algorithm, to a certain class that is sufficient for closed loops, and has investigated many cases where a closed loop does not emerge. We focus here on the conditions required for a closed loop, and will see that there is in fact a lot more freedom in terms of which angles we can use.

In particular, we will see next that as long as two successive "gadgets" form an angle in $\mathbb{Q}$ different from $0+360n, n \in \mathbb{Z}$, we get a closed loop.

3.2 Some remarks

Note that in this post, all angles are in degrees. (Sorry, no pies.) We'd also like a better system since writing "$x+360n, n \in \mathbb{Z}$" every time is very cumbersome.

Remembering what we just learned, it shouldn't be hard to appreciate the equivalence between two angles differing by a multiple of $360$ as yet another equivalence relation. The resulting set of equivalence classes can be denoted $\mathbb{R}/360\mathbb{Z}$ (a quotient set) and is a ring. A shortcoming of the notation $[a]$ is that when we have two different quotient sets with common representatives, it gets hard to tell which quotient set the equivalence class comes from. We will be talking about both $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$ and $\mathbb{R}/360\mathbb{Z}$ concurrently so this will be a problem. Let's solve it by consistently using representatives in $[0, 360)$ and omitting the square brackets when we work with angles. (This is the meaning of "$angle$ modulo $360$" in the algorithm.)

Also note that unlike the things we talked about in the previous section, which are standard in the world of mathematics, a lot of things hereafter probably won't make much sense to people who have never read this post. 9

3.3 Curves and gadgets

Define a curve to be a bijective contiuous function $\mathcal{C}: [0,1] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^2$. (For those unfamiliar with the concept of continuity, think of a curve as something that is drawn without lifting the tip of the pen off the paper.)

A curve is by definition directed: given $\mathcal{C}$, we refer to $\mathcal{C}(0)$ as the inlet and $\mathcal{C}(1)$ as the outlet. If $\mathcal{C}(0) = \mathcal{C}(1)$ we say $\mathcal{C}$ is a loop or closed loop.

Given two curves $\mathcal{C}$ and $\mathcal{D}$ such that $\mathcal{C}(1) = \mathcal{D}(0)$, we can form their concatenation, denoted $\mathcal{C}+\mathcal{D}$, where \begin{align*} (\mathcal{C}+\mathcal{D})(t) = \begin{cases} \mathcal{C}(2t), & 0 \le t < 1/2 \\ \mathcal{D}(2(t-1/2)), & 1/2 \le t \le 1 \\ \end{cases} \end{align*} (11)

Given a curve $\mathcal{C}$, we say another curve $\mathcal{D}$ is a subcurve of $\mathcal{C}$, or $\mathcal{D} \subseteq \mathcal{C}$, if there is a monotonically increasing function $\alpha: [a,b] \rightarrow [0,1]$ such that $\mathcal{C}(t) = \mathcal{D}(\alpha(t)), \forall t \in [a,b] \subseteq [0, 1]$.

When we have $\mathcal{C} \subseteq \mathcal{D}$ and $\mathcal{D} \subseteq \mathcal{C}$, we say $\mathcal{C}$ and $\mathcal{D}$ are equivalent, or $\mathcal{C} \equiv \mathcal{D}$ (yeah this is actually an equivalence relation).

A function $T: \mathbb{R}^2 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^2$ is called a translation if there is some $(u,v) \in \mathbb{R}^2$ such that $T(x,y) = (u+x, v+y)$.

For $\theta \in [0, 360)$, define $R[\theta]: \mathbb{R}^2 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^2$, $R[\theta] = (xcos\theta^{\circ} - ysin\theta^{\circ}, xsin\theta^{\circ} + ycos\theta^{\circ})$. This gives us a parameterized family of rotations.

Given a curve $\mathcal{C}$, a subcurve $\mathcal{G}$ is called a generating gadget of $\mathcal{C}$ if there is a sequence of functions $U_n: \mathbb{R}^2 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^2, n = 0, \cdots, m$ with $m > 0$ such that:

  • there exist $\theta \in [0, 360)$ and for each $n$ two translations $T_n$ and $T_n'$ such that $U_n = T_n R[n\theta] T_n'$, and
  • $\mathcal{C} \equiv \sum_{n=0}^m U_i(\mathcal{G})$

In this case we say $\mathcal{C}$ has a decomposition (note that in general we do not have a unique decomposition) or is decomposable into gadgets (with each $U_i(\mathcal{G})$ being a gadget), and we refer to $\theta$ as the gangle of the decomposition.

If a curve is not decomposable into gadgets, we say it's atomic.

3.4 The inscribed circle and the central angle

Given a curve decomposable into gadgets with gangle $\theta$ and a generating gadget $\mathcal{G}$ such that $\mathcal{G}(0) \neq \mathcal{G}(1)$, we form a new curve, the itinerary, consisting of successive line segments, called steps, through successive gadgets, such that each line segment connects the inlet to the outlet of its corresponding gadget.

This drawing shows how the two gadgets in the previous example become two steps in the itinerary:

We make the following observations:

  • Because both translations and rotations are isometries, which don't make things bigger or smaller, the steps of an itinerary are always equal in length.
  • Because rotating a gadget is the same as rotating its corresponding step, the direction of a step relative to its predecessor is constant throughout the itinerary and is simply $\theta$.

Given these nice properties, for a decomposition with $\theta \neq 0$ and $\theta \neq 180$, we can find a unique circle tangent to all steps in the itinerary using some simple geometry:

  • find the centers of two successive steps, and then
  • for each of the two steps, draw a line perpendicular to the step through its center.

The two perpendicular lines always intersect at one point, and that's the center of the inscribed circle, as shown in this drawing:

Using a little more geometry we can see that the central angle $\eta$ as shown is identical to $\theta$, and the radius $r$ of the inscribed circle can be expressed in terms of the step length $a$ and $\theta$: since $(1/2)a/r = tan(\eta^{\circ}/2)$, we can substitute $\eta = \theta$ and rearrange to get $r = a/(2tan(\theta^{\circ}/2))$.

This expression explains why we have excluded $\theta = 0$ and $\theta = 180$: as $\theta \rightarrow 0$ we have $r \rightarrow \infty$, and when $\theta = 180$ we have $r=0$. Neither case gives us an inscribed circle. However, we don't mind having $\theta \in (180, 360)$. This corresponds to a "left turn" just as $\theta \in (0, 180)$ gives us a "right turn". When $r$ is negative, we can tell it's an inscribed circle resulting from an itinerary of successive left turns.

What should be obvious from the drawing is this: whether we get a closed loop is determined by the central angle, and thus, $\theta$. This is the key takeaway of this subsection, and we will look at the details shortly.

3.5 Singularities, removable or not

In our context, there is a conceivable (but not necessarily attainable) pathological situation not covered by the previous section: we assumed $\mathcal{G}(0) \neq \mathcal{G}(1)$, since otherwise the step length of the itinerary would be zero, but what if a gadget is itself a loop? In this limiting case the gadgets revolve around a center of their overlapping inlets/outlets, and the amount of rotation by a single gadget is still $\theta$. So zero step length by itself doesn't create extra problems (it's a "removable singularity"), and we simply apply the same analysis to $\theta$ as we would do other cases.

For $\theta$, as explained in the previous section, there are two singularities: $0$ and $180$.

When $\theta = 0$, then there are two possibilities:

  • $\mathcal{G}(0) = \mathcal{G}(1)$: the gadgets completely overlap each other (and we get loops over loops since a single gadget already gives us a closed loop)
  • $\mathcal{G}(0) \neq \mathcal{G}(1)$: the gadgets advance down a straight line and don't form a closed loop.

This post has already got longer than planned, so there will be no further investigation concerning this "essential singularity". Interested readers are encouraged to proceed with their own investigation, and let me know with an issue (or email me if we are already connected) if they discover anything interesting.

In case $\theta = 180$, two gadgets are enough to close the loop. So this is another "removable singularity". Try drawing in your head what you would get with $ord=3$, $gen=2$ and $angle=90$, or $ord=4$, $gen=1$ and $angle=60$, and then test in the demo and see if the result is expected.

3.6 What values of $\theta$ give us a closed loop?

If we restrict $\theta$ to $\mathbb{Z}$, then by 2.5.4.3, for any $\theta$ such that $0 < \theta < 360$, we have $ord(\theta)[\theta] = [0]$ in $\mathbb{Z}/360\mathbb{Z}$. (The exclusion of $0$ is not necessitated by group theory but is due to the reasons given above.) In other words, if we turn $ord([\theta]) = 360/gcd(\theta, 360)$ (per 2.5.4.2) times, the total angle will be a multiple of $360$, and we will be back to where we started.

However, since we can combine multiple gadgets to form a single gadget, $\theta$ is also free to assume fractional values. In fact, for $\theta \in \mathbb{Q} \bigcap (0,360)$, there is $d \in \mathbb{Z}, d>0$ such that $\theta d \in \mathbb{Z}$. If $360 | \theta d$ then we know $d$ iterations of the gadget already closes the loop. Otherwise $d \cdot ord([\theta d])$ iterations of the gadget is sufficient to close the loop, again by 2.5.4.3.

We certainly cannot expand the range any further, since no multiple of an irrational number can equal a multiple of 360. Therefore $\mathbb{Q} \bigcap (0,360)$ is the exact range where $\theta$ guarantees a closed loop.

I didn't bother supporting arbitrary fractions (most would be excessive for the browser anyway), but those who are interested can edit the source code and see what patterns we get with $\theta = 1/3, 1/5, 1/7$, etc.

3.7 What values of $angle$ give us a closed loop?

We have talked about the gangle and the central angle. We now look at the $angle$ argument of the algorithm. What choices do we have here?

Depends.

Let $\zeta=angle$ and $m=ord$. We consider the cyclic group $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$. Let $g=gen$, the algorithm draws successive atomic gadgets each with $ord([g])-1$ line segments: $ord([g])$ can be calculated using 2.5.4.2, and minus $1$ because we exclude length $0$. The gangle $\theta$ thus equals $(ord(g)-1)\zeta$ modulo $360$. The algorithm determines $\theta$'s acceptability according to the previous section, and gives up if this check fails.

The exclusion of $(180, 360)$ is for a simple reason: symmetry. We have already talked about "left turn" vs "right turn" for different values of $\theta$ above. Same story here. (Btw, math and computer graphics use different conventions and handedness, and we are using the CG convention here.)

The exclusion of $180$ is aesthetic: the pattern wouldn't look nice. However, it's not hard to decide when we get a closed loop in this case without all the geometry above.

Let $s=ord([g])$. When $s$ is even, we have: \begin{align*} S_e &:= \sum_{n=1}^{s/2-1} (2n)g \\ S_o &:= \sum_{n=0}^{s/2-1} (2n+1)g \end{align*} (12)

Since $S_o-S_e = g + (s/2-1)g = (s/2)g$, by 2.5.4.4, $[S_o-S_e] \neq [0]$. When $s$ is odd, we have: \begin{align*} S_e &:= \sum_{n=1}^{(s-1)/2} (2n)g \\ S_o &:= \sum_{n=1}^{(s-1)/2} (2n-1)g \end{align*} (13)

Since $S_e - S_o = (s-1)/2\cdot g$, again by 2.5.4.4, $[S_o-S_e] \neq [0]$.

Thus, drawing an atomic gadget always results in a net displacement of the drawing head. If $s$ is even, we draw an odd number of line segments for an atomic gadget: forward, backward, …, forward, resulting in a net displacement forward. The next atomic gadget is then: backward, forward, …, backward, resulting a net displace backward by the same amount. The two cancel each other so the total displacement is zero and we are back where we started. On the other hand, if $s$ is odd, the net displacement is always forward. The drawing head is forward by the same amount after drawing each gadget.
