Quantum observer paradox

2024/03/28

Suppose we have a Stern-Gerlach device exhibiting particle-like behavior 1, such that in the $x$ direction, we have a complete basis: $\{ |measure_{+x}\rangle, |measure_{-x}\rangle \}$, akin to a spin-$\frac{1}{2}$ particle, which has $\{ |spin_{+x}\rangle, |spin_{-x}\rangle \}$.

Suppose the Stern-Gerlach device is configured as in Feynman's beautiful exposition 2 to block the lower beam, allowing only particles that take the upper route to fly through.

Now, what happens when a spin-$\frac{1}{2}$ particle in the state of $|spin_{+z}\rangle$ meets our "quantum" Stern-Gerlach device in the state of $|measure_{+z}\rangle$?

Since the spin is "parallel" (so to speak) with the direction of the magnetic field, the particle takes the upper route and flies through with 100% probability, as it would do with a "classical" Stern-Gerlach device.

But wait.

What if we look at the interaction in the $x$ direction?

Taking tensor product and expanding in the respective bases we get 3: \begin{align*} &|spin_{+z}\rangle \otimes |measure_{+z}\rangle \\ &= \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}(|spin_{+x}\rangle + |spin_{-x}\rangle) \\ &\otimes \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}(|measure_{+x}\rangle + |measure_{-x}\rangle) \\ &= \frac{1}{2}|spin_{+x}\rangle \otimes |measure_{+x}\rangle \\ &+ \frac{1}{2}|spin_{-x}\rangle \otimes |measure_{+x}\rangle \\ &+ \frac{1}{2}|spin_{+x}\rangle \otimes |measure_{-x}\rangle \\ &+ \frac{1}{2}|spin_{-x}\rangle \otimes |measure_{-x}\rangle \end{align*}

The spin and the magnetic field are "parallel" in two and "antiparallel" in the other two of the four equally likely states. So the particle flies through with 50% probability.

Oops!

I came up with this paradox last year during a debate with a theoretical physicist friend over the interpretation of quantum mechanics. He "resolved" the paradox by doing another change of basis back into the $z$ direction, which I found problematic: it's like applying the Lorenz transformation twice and declaring there is no time dilation or length contraction.

I recently consulted another theoretical physicist who kindly analyzed the paradox and provided his resolution: spin and a magnetic field are different things and cannot be treated with the same mathematics. I don't know enough physics to agree or disagree, but it does seem to me that if we allow waves to propagate to macroscopic things, some kind of paradox like this is inevitable (I had a more elaborate justification last year but unfortunately can't remember the details).

Since I now have a blog and need some content for this month, I have decided to put the paradox here. Hope it inspires some interesting discussions and thoughtful reexamination of what we take for granted, like how a "measurement" works, or why particles "spin".
