Ending a detour: [not] solving an open problem in 17 days

2024/03/30

1 The detour problem

The statement given by Antoine Amarilli is rephrased here:

Given a directed graph $G = (V,E)$, a source $s \in V$ and a target $t \in V$ reachable from $s$, decide if there a simple directed path from $s$ to $t$ whose length (in terms of edge count) is greater than that of the shortest path from $s$ to $t$. Does this problem have a polynomial time algorithm?

It is mentioned in this 2023 paper by four MIT scholars (look for "it remains open whether k-Longest Detour is in P even for the special case of k = 1!").

2 The PTIME solution

2.1 Definitions

Let $G = (V,E)$ be a directed graph and $s,t \in V$.

Define a bundle $\mathcal{B}_{s,t}$ as the subgraph of $G$ containing exactly all shortest paths from $s$ to $t$.

For a subgraph $H = (V_H, E_H)$, define $\partial H$ as the set of edges in $E$ with one end in $V_H$ and the other end in $V-V_H$, and define $G-H$ as $(V-V_H, E-E_H-\partial H)$. For $u \in V$, define $H + u$ as the subgraph with vertexes $V_H \cup \{u\}$ and whose edge set is the union of $E_H$, $\{(u,v) \in E| v \in H\}$ and $\{(v,u) \in E| v \in H\}$.

For $u,v \in V_H$, define $dist_H(u,v)$ as the distance (possibly $\infty$) from $u$ to $v$ in $H$. The subscript $H$ is omitted if $H = G$.

For $s, t_1, t_2 \in V_H$, define 122-augmented $H^+_{s \rightarrow t_1, t_2}$ as the graph obtained following these steps:

  • replace each $v \in V_H - \{s\}$ with $v_{in}, v_{out}$ connected with an edge $(v_{in}, v_{out})$, and connect all inbound edges to $v_{in}$ and outbound edges from $v_{out}$
  • add a new sink $t^+$ and connect from $t_{1,out}$ and $t_{2,out}$

The construction of 221-augmented $H^+_{s_1, s_2 \rightarrow t}$ is similar except we add a new source $s^+$ before the two sources (instead of a new sink $t^+$ after the two sinks).

2.3 Proof sketch

It shouldn't be hard to see why the algorithm runs in polynomial time. For example, $\mathcal{B}_{s,t}$ can be built using a forward bfs with depth $dist(s,t)$ and then a backward bfs. Also, if the algorithm reports true, then it has definitely found a simple detour (since part of it lies outside $\mathcal{B}_{s,t}$).

If there is a simple detour, it must have at least one segment entirely outside $\mathcal{B}_{s,t}$ except the start $u$ and finish $v$. Then we have $dist_{G-\mathcal{B}_{s,t}+u+v}(u,v) < \infty$. If $u$ is further away from $s$ than $v$, that is, the segment travels "backward", things get interesting. What if there is no way to go (inside $\mathcal{B}_{s,t}$) from $s$ to $u$ without visiting $v$, or from $v$ to $t$ without visiting $u$? In this case, the detour must have (outside $\mathcal{B}_{s,t}$) skipped $v$ on its way from $s$ to $u$, or be skipping $u$ on its way from $v$ to $t$, so we have another shorter simple detour that doesn't give us this difficulty. WLOG, we only need to concern ourselves with detours that have a single segment outside $\mathcal{B}_{s,t}$ without this difficulty, and the algorithm handles them properly.

3 Ending a long detour

Mar 13, while looking for a volunteer to beta test my svg latex solution, I came across Antoine Amarilli's big list of open problems in theoretical computer science.

Having previously spent five of the best years of my life trying to solve a well-known open problem (FTR, the ≠ 1) and come back empty-handed, I was cautious about trying a new one. After all, those five years could have been better spent on things like AI, crypto and/or distributed computing, where I probably would have enjoyed some success, both scholarly and financial.

Mark Twain once said: "Twenty years from now you will be more disappointed by the things you didn't do than the things you did." It hasn't been twenty years but I already feel the opposite about my choice 2. That's why the "Detour problem" caught my eyes as I glanced through the open problems: probably no name is more descriptive of what I am dealing with in real life!

As with the previous open problem, getting in was easy: all it took was a wrong solution 3 that made it look like a final solution was within reach. But getting out was hard.

Fortunately, things seem to have worked out this time, exactly when I need a proof that I am still fit for scholarly pursuits. Having done research on various things over the years, I always enjoyed the hermit style: research but never publish. (Though I had other reasons, I think Black and Scholes have one explanation why somebody might want to withhold their research from public view.) This time however, circumstances have changed, and the choice is between publish and perish, so let me RUSH to publish!

It goes without saying that the solution has not yet been peer-reviewed, so be careful if you decide to rely on it.

4 The detours in ending the detour

4.1 The missing possibilities

The original "simple solution" I came up with quickly (thanks to some leetcode experience last year 4) without thinking about "simple" was something like:

Run Floyd-Warshall to obtain $dist$
For $u,v \in V$:
  If $dist(s,t) < dist(s,u) + dist(v,t) + 1 < \infty$:
      Return True
Return False

Here is an attempt to fix it ($G^r$ means the transpose of $G$):

Run Floyd-Warshall to obtain $dist$
For $v \in V$:
  Calculate $\mathcal{B}_{s,v}$, $\mathcal{B}_{v,t}$ and their transposes using $dist$
For $v \in V$, $v \notin \mathcal{B}_{s,t}$:
  If VDJ-221$(\mathcal{B}_{s,v} \cup \mathcal{B}_{v,t}^r, s, t, v)$:
    Return True
Return False
